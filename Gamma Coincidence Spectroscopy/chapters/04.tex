\section{Energy Calibration}
To correctly scale the energy axes of the spectra for later sources, an energy calibration is performed for both detectors. It is done by measuring the photopeak positions of $^{22}$Na, $^{137}$Cs, and $^{57}$Co sources and plotting them over the well known theoretical values of the corresponding transitions, giving a linear fit. These fits for the NaI scintillator and the Ge semiconductor detector respectively are depicted in figures \ref{fig:linefit_ge} and \ref{fig:linefit_nai}. To determine the exact positions of the photopeaks, gaussian fits were used. The fits for all three calibration sources and both detectors are depicted in fig. \ref{fig:photopeaks_gaussian_fits}.

\section{Coincidence Analysis}
\subsection{$\Delta T$ calibration using  $^{22}$Na coincidences}
To find a suitable time difference window between the signal of both detectors that correspond to likely coincident signals, a calibration is done using  a $^{22}$Na source, whose signal is perfectly coincident by its $\beta^+$-decay which leads to an electron-positron annihilation and in turn to the antiparallel emission of two photons, each with an energy of \SI{511}{\kilo eV}. A histogram of the measured time delay between both detectors signals is shown in figure \ref{fig:Na22_coincidence}.

\begin{figure}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\linewidth]{Gamma Coincidence Spectroscopy/coincidence_fit_22Na.jpg}
    \caption{Time delay of a coincident signal}
    \label{fig:Na22_coincidence}
\end{figure}

A Gaussian fitted to the histogram yields a mean of $\mu = -86.25$ and a standard deviation of $\sigma = 0.71$. 
The delay time window corresponding to a likely coincident signal, is set to the interval of $\mu \pm 2\sigma$, i.e. 
$[-86.67, -84.83]$, which is further used for the coincidence analysis $^{60}$Co.

\subsection{Coincidence analysis of $^{60}$Co}

A coincidence measurement of a $^{60}$Co-source was recorded. The measured spectra for both detectors are visible in figure \ref{fig:60Coa}. Due to an electronics issue that occurred during the measurement, the spectrum recorded by the Germanium detector contains a peak at $\SI{1500}{kEv}$ among other shifted features, such as a third Compton edge. This is remedied by cutting away all data measured after this electronics issue, resulting in a dataset that has $\SI{48}{\%}$
the size of the original dataset. This dataset is further filtered to only contain data within determined the delay time window, cutting the dataset down further to $\SI{8}{\%}$ of the original dataset.
